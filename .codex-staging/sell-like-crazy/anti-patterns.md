# Anti-Patterns

These mistakes regularly lower response, damage trust, or attract the wrong leads.

## 1. Clever but unclear messaging

Problem:
The copy sounds polished but the buyer cannot tell what is being offered.

Do instead:
Lead with audience, outcome, and next step before trying to sound distinctive.

## 2. Talking about the brand more than the buyer

Problem:
The message centers company passion, mission, or adjectives instead of customer stakes.

Do instead:
Frame the copy around the buyer's problem, desired result, and proof.

## 3. Feature dumping without outcome translation

Problem:
The reader gets a list of capabilities without understanding why they matter.

Do instead:
Translate each feature into a practical result, saved time, lowered risk, or easier workflow.

## 4. Broad claims with no mechanism

Problem:
Big promises feel generic or unbelievable when the method is missing.

Do instead:
Explain the approach, process, or difference that makes the claim credible.

## 5. Weak or generic CTA

Problem:
Buttons like `Learn more` or `Submit` hide the value of the next step.

Do instead:
Name the action and the payoff clearly.

## 6. Fake urgency or fake scarcity

Problem:
Manufactured deadlines damage credibility and make the copy feel manipulative.

Do instead:
Use only real deadlines, real capacity, or real consequences of delay.

## 7. Invented proof

Problem:
Made-up testimonials, logos, or metrics create legal and trust risk.

Do instead:
Use real proof, or recommend proof formats the business should gather.

## 8. Asking for too much too soon

Problem:
The page requests a purchase, demo, or long form completion before enough value is established.

Do instead:
Match the ask to buyer intent and reduce commitment at earlier stages.

## 9. Trying to speak to everyone

Problem:
The offer becomes softer and less believable when it is written for every possible audience.

Do instead:
Choose one buyer segment, one job to be done, and one core promise at a time.

## 10. Hiding the offer details

Problem:
Buyers cannot tell what they actually get, how it works, or whether it is a fit.

Do instead:
State deliverables, scope, timing, support, and next steps plainly.

## 11. Leaving objections to the imagination

Problem:
The copy avoids price, effort, switching cost, risk, or implementation concerns.

Do instead:
Address common objections directly with proof or explanation.

## 12. One-shot funnel thinking

Problem:
The strategy assumes the buyer will convert on the first touch.

Do instead:
Design the follow-up path: reminders, proof, FAQs, nurture, retargeting, or deadline messages.

## 13. Optimizing design before message

Problem:
Teams tweak colors, layouts, and minor details while the value proposition stays weak.

Do instead:
Fix message clarity, offer strength, and proof placement before cosmetic changes.

## 14. Overpromising speed or certainty

Problem:
Aggressive claims may increase clicks but reduce trust and conversion quality.

Do instead:
Use confident but defensible language tied to real conditions and outcomes.

## 15. Confusing complexity with sophistication

Problem:
Long explanations, too many options, or bloated funnels make the buyer work too hard.

Do instead:
Simplify the path, reduce decision load, and make the next step easy to understand.
