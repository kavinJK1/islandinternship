# Conversion Framework

Use this sequence to diagnose, rebuild, or optimize almost any sales or marketing asset.

## 1. Goal and stage

Clarify the primary action first.

- top of funnel: stop the scroll, earn the click, capture the lead
- middle of funnel: build belief, reduce doubt, move to demo or trial
- bottom of funnel: resolve objections, justify price, create confidence to buy
- post-purchase: expand usage, renew, upsell, ask for referrals or reviews

If the goal is unclear, the copy usually becomes generic.

## 2. Buyer definition

Define the buyer with enough detail to make the message feel chosen, not broad.

- who they are
- what they are trying to achieve
- what is frustrating them now
- what they have already tried
- what they are afraid of
- what could stop them from acting today

Good conversion copy names the moment the buyer is in, not just their demographic label.

## 3. Problem intensity

Surface the cost of staying where they are.

- wasted time
- lost revenue
- missed opportunity
- avoidable complexity
- risk, stress, or uncertainty

The goal is not melodrama. The goal is to make the problem concrete.

## 4. Desired outcome

Describe the result the buyer wants in plain language.

- what changes after they say yes
- how quickly value appears
- what improves operationally, financially, or emotionally
- what "better" looks like in practical terms

Strong messaging turns ambition into a visible before-and-after.

## 5. Mechanism

Explain why this offer is different or believable.

- what the method is
- why it works
- why it is easier, faster, safer, or more reliable than alternatives
- what the buyer gets access to that they did not have before

Specific mechanism beats inflated claims.

## 6. Offer architecture

Build the offer so it feels easy to evaluate.

- core deliverable
- who it is for and not for
- what is included
- timeframe, scope, or access level
- price framing
- bonuses or support
- guarantee or risk reversal
- urgency or deadline if it is real

If the buyer cannot summarize the offer in one sentence, it is probably too muddy.

## 7. Proof and trust

Show why the claim deserves belief.

- customer results
- case studies
- product demos
- quantified examples
- credibility markers
- process transparency

Proof should answer the buyer's next doubt.

## 8. Friction and CTA

Make the next step obvious and worthwhile.

- one primary CTA
- clear action language
- low-effort path to start
- explanation of what happens next
- no unnecessary form fields or hidden steps

Confusion and friction often destroy otherwise solid offers.

## 9. Follow-up system

Most conversions happen after the first touch.

- lead capture or retargeting path
- reminder sequence
- objection-handling emails
- proof reinforcement
- deadline sequence when appropriate
- handoff from marketing to sales or onboarding

If there is no follow-up, performance depends too much on first-visit intent.

## 10. Optimization loop

Test message and structure before minor design tweaks.

- first test the headline and value proposition
- then test the offer framing
- then test proof placement
- then test CTA wording and page friction
- track the one metric tied to the goal

## Fast diagnosis map

Use this when the user needs quick triage.

- strong traffic, weak conversion: message match, offer, proof, or CTA problem
- high clicks, weak opt-in: landing page clarity or form friction problem
- strong opt-in, weak sales: lead quality, offer fit, or follow-up problem
- many demos, few closes: objection handling, proof, pricing, or qualification problem
- strong interest, slow action: urgency, risk reversal, or next-step clarity problem

## Minimum output standard

Every recommendation should cover these five items:

- audience
- promise
- proof
- offer
- next step
